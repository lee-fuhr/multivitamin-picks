How to choose a multivitamins

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose a multivitamin backed by third-party testing (USP, NSF, or ConsumerLab), with nutrients in forms your body can actually use – like methylated B12 and chelated minerals – and avoid megadoses that exceed the tolerable upper intake level. Match the formula to your life stage and gender, and always check the serving size to ensure you’re not taking more than needed.

Why you might need a multivitamin

A multivitamin isn’t a magic bullet, but it can help fill the gaps in a less-than-perfect diet. Most adults miss key nutrients like vitamin D, magnesium, and omega-3s, and a well-chosen multivitamin provides a safety net without replacing whole foods. That said, it’s important to have realistic expectations. A multivitamin won’t make up for poor eating habits, and it can’t prevent chronic disease on its own. Think of it as a low-cost insurance policy for days when your meals aren’t as balanced as you’d like.

Key nutrients to look for (and what to skip)

A good multivitamin should include vitamin D (as D3), methylated B12 and folate (methylcobalamin and methylfolate), magnesium (in a well-absorbed form like glycinate or citrate), zinc, and a modest amount of vitamin C. Avoid formulas that push nutrients into super-high doses – more than 200% of the Daily Value – since excesses can cause side effects or interfere with other medications. Many multivitamins also add iron or calcium, but these aren’t right for everyone. Men and postmenopausal women often don’t need extra iron, and large calcium doses can compete with magnesium absorption. Look for a formula that matches your specific needs, or choose one without these minerals and get them from food instead.

Forms matter: tablets, capsules, gummies, or liquids?

Tablets are the most common and usually the most affordable, but they can be hard to digest and may contain binders that hinder absorption. Capsules are easier on the stomach and often use higher-quality ingredients, though they tend to cost a bit more per serving. Gummies are popular for taste and convenience, but they almost always contain added sugar or sugar alcohols, and they rarely provide the full spectrum of nutrients you’d get from a tablet or capsule. Liquids can be great for people who have trouble swallowing pills, but they often require a larger dose and may degrade faster. In general, capsules offer the best balance of bioavailability and convenience.

Gender and age-specific formulas: do they make a difference?

Yes – but not always in the way marketing suggests. Women of childbearing age need more iron and folate, while men typically benefit from lower iron levels and may want added lycopene or zinc for prostate health. Senior formulas often include higher vitamin D and B12, plus extra calcium for bone density. However, a ‘men’s’ or ‘women’s’ label isn’t a guarantee of quality. You should still check the nutrient amounts and forms on the supplement facts panel. A generic multivitamin with appropriate levels could serve you just as well, especially if you’re not in a high-need life stage.

Quality markers: third-party testing and certifications

The supplement industry is lightly regulated, so a seal from USP, NSF International, or ConsumerLab is your best assurance that what’s on the label matches what’s in the bottle. These organizations test for purity, potency, and the absence of contaminants like heavy metals. Without such certification, you’re relying entirely on the brand’s reputation. Look for companies that openly share their testing results or list their certifications on the product page. A few extra dollars for a verified product is a worthwhile investment in safety.

How to read a label: serving size and bioavailability

Always check the ‘serving size’ – some multivitamins ask you to take two or three pills per serving, which multiplies the cost and calorie count. Also look at the form of each nutrient. For example, magnesium oxide is poorly absorbed, while magnesium glycinate or citrate is better. Similarly, folic acid is a synthetic form that some people can’t use, whereas methylfolate is bioactive. Don’t get dazzled by ingredient lists that stretch across two rows. More isn’t always better. Focus on the nutrients most Americans lack (vitamin D, magnesium, vitamin B12) and ignore boutique additions like herbs or enzymes unless they’re backed by solid evidence.

Frequently asked questions

Can I take a multivitamin on an empty stomach?

It depends on the form. Capsules and dry tablets are generally fine for most people, but gummies or chewables may cause mild nausea. If the multivitamin contains iron or zinc, taking it with a small meal can reduce stomach upset and improve absorption.

Should I choose a multivitamin with iron?

Only if you actually need extra iron. Men and postmenopausal women typically don’t, and excess iron can be harmful over time. Women who menstruate, pregnant women, and people with diagnosed deficiencies are the main groups that benefit from including iron in their daily multivitamin.

Are expensive multivitamins worth the extra cost?

Not always. A mid-priced multivitamin from a reputable brand that uses high-quality nutrient forms and third-party testing is often a smarter buy than a luxury bottle with flashy packaging. Price alone doesn’t guarantee quality, so focus on certifications and ingredient transparency.

How long does it take for a multivitamin to work?

You won’t feel an immediate effect. For most people, it takes several weeks of consistent use to notice changes in energy, mood, or immunity, and even then, the effects are subtle. Multivitamins work best as a long-term habit, not a quick fix.

Can I take a multivitamin with other supplements?

Generally yes, but be mindful of overlapping nutrients. For example, if you already take a separate vitamin D or magnesium supplement, a multivitamin may push you over the safe upper limit. Check the total from all sources and consider a multivitamin with lower doses if you’re combining it with other products.

What does ‘methylated’ mean on a vitamin label?

Methylated vitamins – like methylcobalamin (B12) and methylfolate (B9) – are already in the active form your body can use directly. Many people have genetic variations that make it harder to convert synthetic vitamins into active forms, so methylated versions can be more effective, especially for older adults and those with MTHFR mutations.

In shortThe best multivitamin is the one you’ll actually take consistently, but quality matters more than convenience. Look for a third-party tested formula with appropriate forms and dosages for your age and gender, and don’t hesitate to ask a doctor or pharmacist if you’re unsure about a specific ingredient. A well-chosen multivitamin is a small step that can make a real difference over time.
